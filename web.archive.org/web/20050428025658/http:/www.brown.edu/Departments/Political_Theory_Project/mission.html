he mission of the Political Theory Project is to invigorate and deepen the discussion of normatively charged political issues at Brown, and and within the academy more generally.

The health of free societies rests ultimately on the degree of seriousness and care with which citizens form their political viewpoints. Having contact with teachers, lecturers and colleagues who sincerely hold diverse opinions on fundamental political values is essential to this. But within many universities today, the spectrum of intellectual opinion is dangerously narrow. Collectivist and quasi-socialist political perspectives abound within many universities, while important rival perspectives on political reality---notably, individualist, market-oriented or religious ones---rarely are attended to sympathetically.

Brown University is no exception to this trend. A recent New York Times described the lack of intellectual diversity at Brown generally and in the undergraduate curriculum in particular. As one student quoted in that article reported: "I have been asked to read Marx four times at Brown. Adam Smith? Not even once."

There is no easy diagnosis for this state of affairs in American education. But it seems clear that the disconnection between the political discourse within universities and that of the wider society stems in part from disciplinary factionalization---and especially a factionalization between humanistic and social scientific approaches. That factionalization has resulted in a state of affairs inn which normative theorizing about politics is all too often conducted in a purely theoretical, "critical" or even literary vacuum. Critical thinking about politics threatens to become a wholly interior, even circular, enterprise. It adverts to injustices and makes claims of oppression, but does not propose concrete alternative systems of rule whose efficacy might be empirically tested.

The Political Theory Project is founded on the belief that critical thinking about politics must cash out into testable claims about our social world. Responsible criticism of political institutions must be conducted in light of economic reality and the findings of mainstream social science. These disciplines provide ballast to political discourse. They ensure that the formation of each student's political opinions be a result of considered, well-rounded decision---grounded in the complexities of historical and institutional reality---rather than a mere expression of intellectual fashion.

The Political Theory Project supports scholarship that is intimately concerned with how political ideas actually work in the world: providing prosperity, opportunity and freedom for the citizens of the societies that affirm them. This concern with theories that work does not in itself commit the Political Theory Project to any particular ideological orientation. We are interested in ideas and are committed to searching out insights from whatever parts of the political spectrum where we might find them. Our interest in connecting normative theory to actual practice, though, does direct our attention in the first instance to a set of norms that have a record of providing modern societies with a measure of stability, wealth and well-being: individual rights, the rule of law, private property and systems of market exchange, religious liberty, freedoms of speech and association, democratic self-governance, and the ideal of voluntarism in human affairs.

These norms are sometimes derisively associated with terms such as "liberalism," "classical liberalism," or even "Western Civilization." At the Political Theory Project we are not interested in such labels. Indeed, discourse at the level of labels is a central characteristic of the internalized, academic forms of discourse to which we mean to provide an alternative. The Political Theory Project is determined to provide space at Brown where students and faculty can go beneath the labels. The Political Theory Project---the courses, lectures, and conferences we sponsor---are places where individuals of good faith, and diverse viewpoints, can come together to untangle the rivalrous roots of political reality for themselves.
