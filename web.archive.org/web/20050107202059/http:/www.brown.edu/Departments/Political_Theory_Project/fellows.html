Carmen Pavel is a graduate research fellow with The Political Theory Project. She is pursuing her Ph.D. in Political Theory in the Department of Political Science at Brown University. Her research focuses on contemporary liberal theory, with special attention given to liberal international norms. Her broader research and teaching interests extend to multiculturalism, theories of rights, ethics and public policy and the interplay of political theory and economics.

Carmen’s dissertation explores the implications of institutional pluralism for liberal international norms. In particular, she asks whether substantive duties of justice for distant others can be derived from domestic liberal values. She argues that the liberalism prevalent in contemporary academic discourse needs to develop a better grounded respect for plural and sometimes conflicting of conceptions of the good life and their diverse institutional expressions and to contain its universalistic ambitions.



Ross Corbett is currently a postdoctoral fellow in the Political Theory Project at Brown University. He teaches the philosophy of the American founding and American constitutional law. He received his B.A. from the University of Maryland, College Park (cum laude, with honors in government and politics), and is presently completing his doctoral dissertation for the University of Toronto (Ph.D. expected in December 2004). His current research focuses on John Locke's Two Treatises of Government. Generally taken to be contain to the standard Seventeenth-Century Whig argument for the rule of law, the Treatises curiously justify something of the opposite: prerogative, or the legitimate authority to act against the law. This observation leads to the following inquiries. Why would extralegal action be legitimate? How can it be reconciled with the rule of law? What is to prevent the wielder of prerogative from using it tyrannically? The Second Treatise in particular is an attempt to grapple with these questions. Locke's handling of these matters is analyzed. Full CV (PDF)



L. Joseph Hebert is Postdoctoral Research Fellow in Political Science at Brown University and a member of Brown's Political Theory Project (2003-2005). He received his B.A. from the University of Maine and his M.A. (1998) and Ph.D. (2004) from the University of Toronto. He is preparing his dissertation, The Liberal Education of Democracy: A Study of Tocqueville's Democracy in America, for publication, and several of his articles are under review at major journals. Courses he has designed and has or will teach at Brown include "Intellectual Liberty and Moral Authority", "Modern Political Thought", and "Comparative Constitutions". In 2004 he received the Brown University Commendation for Teaching Excellence. His specializations are ancient and modern political philosophy and his interests include American Politics; liberty, justice, equality, and human excellence; comparative constitutionalism; religion and politics; human nature; civil society; liberal democracy and globalization.












Adam James Tebble is a Visiting Fellow at the Department of Political Science, Brown University. He received his B.A. from University College London, his M.A. from Birkbeck College, University of London and his Ph.D. from the London School of Economics. His specialisations are in liberal political theory, nationalism and multiculturalism and he has published papers in the journals Political Theory, Economics and Philosophy and Political Studies.
Dr. Tebble is currently completing a book on Hayekian political theory and its relationship to the politics of identity. Discovering Cultural Justice: Identity, Democracy and the Discursive Minimal State extends recent Hayek scholarship to ask what relevance Hayek’s perspective has to contemporary theorising about justice; in particular to the problematic of theorising justice under conditions of cultural diversity. In this regard, Discovering Cultural Justice serves two purposes: to extend our knowledge of Hayek’s corpus and, by way of offering an interpretative rereading of it, to contribute a new perspective to debates about
cultural diversity, the liberal theory of justice and the limits of theorising justice as a practice.

In addition, Dr. Tebble is preparing a paper entitled ‘Exclusion for Democracy’ that discusses the “New Wave” nationalism that has taken hold in many western liberal democracies since the attacks of 11th of September, 2001. This paper and the research project associated with it question whether the traditional understandings of nationalism in either liberal instrumentalist or conservative constitutive terms adequately capture emergent political currents in liberal deomcracy, particularly in the wake of the War on Terror. Mariah Zeisberg's dissertation, The Constitution of Conflict, explores the role of structured conflict in achieving constitutional fidelity. Using the American Constitution, she analyzes three different kinds of constitutional conflict. First, she examines the tension between free speech and national security to explore how political conflict configured around multiple constitutional values can render liberal democratic commitments more secure over the long term. Second, she evaluates the judicial selection process to understand conflict between norms of political and legal decision-making. And finally, she argues that constitutional maintenance requires interbranch conflict, and hence advances a distinctive understanding of executive prerogative in war powers which does not seek to insulate that prerogative from scrutiny by other branches.

Mariah received her B.A. from the University of Texas at Austin, her M.A. from Princeton, and will defend her dissertation this year. She has been awarded
fellowships from the Princeton University Center for Human Values; Harvard University's Government Department; Princeton's Program in American Studies; the Institut for die Wissenschaften vom Menschen; the Institute for Humane Studies; and the Erasmus Institute. Her teaching at both Princeton and Harvard have been recognized as outstanding. At Harvard she received the Certificate of Distinction in Teaching, and at Princeton she was selected as the Politics department nominee for the University's Excellence in Teaching award. She has presented numerous papers at APSA and other conferences. Her research interests lie in political philosophy, especially liberal and democratic theory; philosophy of law; and constitutional theory. Full CV
