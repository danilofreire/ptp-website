Carmen Pavel is a graduate research fellow with The Political Theory Project. She is pursuing her Ph.D. in Political Theory in the Department of Political Science at Brown University. Her research focuses on contemporary liberal theory, with special attention given to liberal international norms. Her broader research and teaching interests extend to multiculturalism, theories of rights, ethics and public policy and the interplay of political theory and economics.
Recently, political philosophers have focused their attention on the implications of moral pluralism for liberal theory. Indeed many argue that there is a strong foundational relationship between moral pluralism and liberalism. Carmen's dissertation , entitled “Pluralism and the Moral Grounds of Liberal Universalism”, argues first, that the supposed foundational link between liberalism and moral pluralism is philosophically untenable. When we disaggregate the scope of liberal theory and further distinguish between several levels of moral pluralism, the link between pluralism and liberalism starts. While pluralism is certainly compatible with liberalism, it does not ground a new defense of liberal theory.









Ross Corbett is a postdoctoral research associate in the Political Theory Project at Brown University. He specializes in the history of political philosophy, and is currently examining John Locke's Two Treatises of Government. While Locke is strongly associated with the rule of law, he justifies violations of the law when necessary. This observation leads to the following inquiries. Why would extralegal action be legitimate? How can it be reconciled with the rule of law? What is to prevent the wielder of prerogative from using it tyrannically? The Second Treatise in particular is an attempt to grapple with these questions.

Ross Corbett teaches courses on the philosophy of the American founding, constitutional law, and Machiavelli. He received his B.A. from the University of Maryland, College Park in 1999, and his Ph.D. from the University of Toronto in 2005. Full CV (PDF)



L. Joseph Hebert is Postdoctoral Research Fellow in Political Science at Brown University and a member of Brown's Political Theory Project (2003-2005). He received his B.A. from the University of Maine and his M.A. (1998) and Ph.D. (2004) from the University of Toronto. He is preparing his dissertation, The Liberal Education of Democracy: A Study of Tocqueville's Democracy in America, for publication, and several of his articles are under review at major journals. Courses he has designed and has or will teach at Brown include "Intellectual Liberty and Moral Authority", "Modern Political Thought", and "Comparative Constitutions". In 2004 he received the Brown University Commendation for Teaching Excellence. His specializations are ancient and modern political philosophy and his interests include American Politics; liberty, justice, equality, and human excellence; comparative constitutionalism; religion and politics; human nature; civil society; liberal democracy and globalization.












Adam James Tebble received his Ph.D. from the Government Department at the London School of Economics in 2003. His specialisations are in liberal political theory, multiculturalism and nationalism and he has published work in the journals Political Theory, Political Studies and Economics and Philosophy. Dr. Tebble is currently completing a book that defends a neutralist ‘epistemological’ account of liberalism as a response to the challenge of cultural diversity. In Discovering Cultural Justice: Identity, Democracy and the State the challenge of diversity is addressed by way of an insight concerning the epistemological rather than teleological rôle of liberal economic institutions first had by Friederich Hayek.
Importantly, it is the conception of the self that underlies this insight and the radically subversive reformulation of the task of justice it gives rise to that allows one to reconnect the concerns of epistemological liberalism to debates about justice, culture and identity, despite the postsocialist cultural turn in politics and the virtual disappearance of rigorously centralised economic planning.

Jeffrey Metzger's dissertation is an interpretation of the psychological teaching of Nietzsche's On the Genealogy of Morals. His research interests focus on the history of political philosophy, particularly Nietzsche and Plato, the conflict between philosophy and revelation, especially in the Western political tradition, politics and literature, and religion and political idealogy in the contemporary Middle East.










Jermaine McCalpin is presently completing work on a PhD in Political Theory in the Department of Political Science (expected May 2006). His primary research interest centers on the nature of justice in transitional societies, which is the subject of his dissertation. Africana political thought, contemporary political theory, democracy and democratization and Caribbean politics all form his major teaching interests.

His dissertation is a normative inquiry into the problems associated with claims of justice in deeply divided societies such as South Africa. It explores various candidate theories of transitional justice and how best to respond to both the imperatives of building a common future while not abrogating the necessity of rectifying historic injustice.











John Phillips is beginning to write a dissertation on what the disciplines of experimental economics and behavioral psychology can plausibly tell us about the price of liberal political philosophic ambitions. His research interests include sovereignty, the relationship between theories of justice and legitimacy, decision making, and the relationship between facts and norms. John also does most of the administrative work for the Political Theory Project.

John's paper "Libertarianism and the Limits of the Reasonable" is currently under review. Navigating the treacherous waters between liberal justice and liberal legitimacy, the paper discusses the place of libertarianism in liberal political thought. It concludes that theories of libertarian justice are compatible with more liberal views on political legitimacy, despite the fact that many libertarian authors hold distinctly illiberal views about political legitimacy.


Mariah Zeisberg is a postdoctoral fellow in political science and a member of Brown's Political Theory Project. She received her B.A. from the University of Texas at Austin (1999) and her M.A. and Ph.D. (2005) from Princeton University. Her dissertation explores the role of structured political conflict in achieving constitutional fidelity. She has been awarded fellowships from the Princeton University Center for Human Values; Harvard University's Government Department; Princeton's Program in American Studies; the Institut for die Wissenschaften vom Menschen; the Institute for Humane Studies; and the Erasmus Institute. Her teaching at Princeton and Harvard have been recognized as outstanding. At Harvard she received the Certificate of Distinction in Teaching, and at Princeton she was selected as the Politics department nominee for the University's Excellence in Teaching award. She has presented numerous papers at APSA and other conferences. Her research interests lie in law and politics; political philosophy, especially liberal and democratic theory; philosophy of law; and constitutional theory. At Brown last year she designed and taught Democratic Theory and Law and Politics; this year she will teach Philosophy of Law. Full CV
