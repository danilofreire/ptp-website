The Philosophy of the American Founding

In what theoretical context was the constitution created? What problems were the Founders attempting to address? A great deal of modern scholarship centers on the influence of philosopher John Locke. Some scholars argue that his was the primary influence. This seminar will examine these claims by comparing John Locke's thought with that of some of the more influential Founders. Enrollment limited to 20. Written permission required.


The Political and Economic Thought of F. A. Hayek

This seminar will introduce Hayek's thought via an examination of the role of liberty in his account of political association. Starting with philosophical fundamentals of thought, will question if his specifically individualist account of political liberty is the result of a generalization of a generalization of a thesis about the nature of the self and limits of its faculty of reason. Enrollment limited to 20.



Intellectual Liberty and Moral Authority (Past Course)

This course explores the relations between individual human reason and political and religious authority. What effect does liberty of the mind have on the way individuals and societies decide what is right and enforce those decisions? We begin with Plato's classic statement in The Apology of Socrates and go on to examine Rene Descartes' vision of a society transformed by reason, Blaise Pascal's critique of Cartesian rationalism in the name of the soul, and Alexis de Tocqueville's analysis of early American "individualism" in light of this philosophic debate. We also consider contemporary accounts of these issues. Students should expect weekly writing assignments and intensive class participation.



The American Ideal: Past, Present and Future (Past Course)

We ask not only what issues from the constellation of political principles were settled by the Conventions, but also what possibilities were (and are) left open to America.

The second part of the seminar uses these principles to gain critical leverage on a range of 21st century American debates. From the mundane to the spectacular, we examine such issues as economic inequality, campaign finance reform, environmental degradation, privacy, and terrorism. Finally, we ask whether the Founders beliefs should continue to hold relevance for us today, or if we think our very different world calls for a break with the past and a reformulation of our most basic political commitments.

Classical Liberal Political Thought

Examines recent Classical Liberal political theory. Will start with Hayek's epistemological defence of classical liberal institutions and Nozick's rights-based account. We will also examine Milton Friedman's well-known economic arguments for individual freedom and contemporary currents in Classical Liberal thought; more specifically, Chandran Kukathas's tolerationist response to cultural diversity. Enrollment limited to 20. Written permission required.

Issues in Democratic Theory (Past Course)

This seminar engages contemporary issues in democratic theory. Topics explored include the meaning of democracy (and the political consequence of different answers to that question); representation and citizenship; democracy and higher law; deliberative democracy; civic engagement; political resistance and oppositional consciousness; and identity politics. Enrollment limited to 20. Written permission required.
